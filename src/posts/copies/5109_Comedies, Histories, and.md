This edition exists in three distinct states: the first includes copies that were sold without Troilus and Cressida; the second contains Troilus and Cressida but without its prologue and with a redundant final page of Romeo and Juliet crossed out by the printer; the third includes a cancel leaf with the Prologue of Troilus on its recto and the first page of the play, reprinted from a new setting of type, on its verso. In none of the states is Troilus included in the "Catalogue" of plays.

Wants πA1 and π1+1 (title leaf); supplied are the leaves bearing the corresponding text in the 2nd Folio (respectively πA1 and πA2). The title leaf from the 2nd Folio has been patched with pieces of printed facsim. to conform to the wording of the 1st Folio and has been mounted. Gathering 3b wanting; supplied in facsim.

The most extensively annotated play is All’s Well that Ends Well, where manicules, marginal markings, and underlining occurs at least every couple pages.

This copy bears yet another example of how owners have left their marks on Folios in unusual ways. In Romeo and Juliet, signatures ee4v-ee5, have a key-shaped rust mark. Why exactly someone would leave a key in a book is difficult to understand, however, as with the scissors mentioned in previous copies, keys are a common household object and might have been the closest thing at hand to use as a bookmark.
