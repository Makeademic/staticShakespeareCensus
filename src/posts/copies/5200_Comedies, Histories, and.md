This edition exists in three distinct states: the first includes copies that were sold without Troilus and Cressida; the second contains Troilus and Cressida but without its prologue and with a redundant final page of Romeo and Juliet crossed out by the printer; the third includes a cancel leaf with the Prologue of Troilus on its recto and the first page of the play, reprinted from a new setting of type, on its verso. In none of the states is Troilus included in the "Catalogue" of plays.

Aa2 (The Winter's Tale): MS by running title "Richard Massie," late seventeenth-century hand; i2 (1 Henry VI) has MS signature "Hannh Williamson" in nineteenth-century hand .... A number of questions [have been] raised about the provenance of this copy..that was given to the Library of Congress in 1936 ... by John Davis Batchelder. A warning from Peter M. VanWingen, the late LC Specialist for the Book Arts, cautioned against trusting Batchelder’s provenance information due to a history of unreliability. A prime example in the case of Folio 142 is the apparent insertion of the bookplate of Viscount Cholmondeley by Batchelder .... The binding that is now found on 142 and the number of leaves present in the volume would seem to match it to the copy described in an auction catalogue Sotheby’s, lot 138, 22 May 1922 .... Batchelder claims he bought copy 142 in 1919 [but] the Sotheby’s copy was the property of Reginald H. Leon, Esq., who is not mentioned in Batchelder’s provenance for the volume.

Portions of this volume are heavily annotated in several different hands from different time periods. In particular The Tempest, 2 Henry VI, Richard III, Troilus and Cressida, Romeo and Juliet, Hamlet, and Othello were of interest to the annotators. Many of the notes focus on the text’s relation to other editions, textual and theatrical history, and criticism.
