A churchman named John Cosin purchased this copy in London, probably in the late 1620s or early 1630s, and took it north to County Durham, where he was prebendary. In 1635, Cosin was appointed Master of Peterhouse, Cambridge. He supported the royalists during the revolution and, in 1644, fled to France along with other supporters of King Charles I. During his exile, the Folio was incorporated into the Peterhouse Library along with his other books, and a Peterhouse Library mark was written on the top right corner of the portrait on the title-page. When the monarchy was restored in 1660, Cosin returned from France and recovered his books; he was named Bishop of Durham, and subsequently built a public episcopal library on Palace Green next to Durham Cathedral. A letter pasted in the volume reported that Bishop Cosin bequeathed the First Folio to the clergy of his diocese in 1672. In 1935 the Cosin Library, and the First Folio with it, were vested in Durham Colleges and in 1963 in the University. But on 10 December 1998, the Durham library’s crown jewels were stolen: not only the First Folio, but also a 14th-century manuscript translation of the New Testament, two works by the 10th-century poet Aelfric, and a 15th-century manuscript of a Chaucer poem. Investigators assumed that the multimillion-pound heist was the work of professional thieves with connections to the international black market. A decade later, Raymond Scott brought the copy into the Folger Shakespeare Library in Washington, D.C. for authentication; the Folio was identified as the copy that had been stolen from Durham University in 1998, and Scott was arrested by the FBI. (Scott’s story that he acquired the book in Cuba was rendered less than compelling when the fact emerged that he lived ten miles from Durham University.) The defendant arrived at his trial in a horse-drawn carriage, dressed all in white, holding a cigar in one hand and a cup of instant noodles in the other, and reciting lines from Shakespeare’s Richard III. The jury found Scott guilty of handling stolen property and transporting stolen property overseas and he was sentenced to eight years' imprisonment. The copy has now been returned to Durham University.
