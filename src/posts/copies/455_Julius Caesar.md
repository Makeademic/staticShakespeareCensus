This edition displays the typographical peculiarities that Bartlett ascribes to the third of the undated quartos. (The fourth of these dates to around 1715 and is therefore not part of the Shakespeare Census; it can be distinguished from the other three by the fact that it collates A-G4 H2, instead of A-H4 like all the others, and by presence of a three-line note about the location of the scene beneath the Dramatis Personae, beginning: "SCENE, for the Three first Acts ..."). This edition can be distinguished from the others by the fact that there is a colon after "Caesar" in line 1 of title. In this edition, p. 15 is usually--but, as noted on specific copies, not always--misnumbered 51. As in the second undated quarto, larger type was used to set C1v-C2r (pp. 18-19) and D2v-D2r (pp. 28-29). For a full discussion of the undated editions of Julius Caesar, see Emma Depledge, forthcoming work.

Catalogue description bound in at front. A letter from Henrietta Bartlett to Walter Thomas Wallace is bound in at front, dated 9 Oct 1912: "My dear Mr Wallace - Many thanks for the full particulars as to your Shakespeare collection which is a nice one even if there is only one item in it which comes within my present scope. If you could have only one quarto, I am glad it is an undated 'Julius Caesar' for I have studied that play bibliographically for two years and am sending you under separate cover the results of my research. My opinion as to the arrangement of editions was approved by Messrs. W. A. White, Beverly Chew and L.S. Livingston here as well as Mr. Pollard and Mr. H. B. Wheatley in England although we are none of us sure as to 'Quarto 5'. [inserted in pencil] ^the one of 60pp. considerably later than mine^  All I know is that none of them could have been printed before 1682 and I think '5' comes after the 1684 one. I am naturally very anxious to see your copy and make a careful examination for the the Census as well as for my own satisfaction. If you would be so very kind as to bring it over to town with you and then let me come down to your office and examine it, I should be much grateful. If you would prefer to bring it up here, I will borrow Mr. White's copies when I go over there on Tuesday next and we can make it absolutely certain as to which edition it belonged to. If it's not too much trouble and you would call me up on the phone (5360 Spring) any morning this week before 12 o'clock, I could arrange about Mr. White's books as it's so much better to make the physical comparison. I shall be out Monday and Tuesday of next week but any time after that will suit me and I will gladly go to you if that suits you better. New copies of 'Julius Caesar' are like glittering El Dorados to me now. Hoping you will be interested too, I am truly yours, Henrietta C. Bartlett."
