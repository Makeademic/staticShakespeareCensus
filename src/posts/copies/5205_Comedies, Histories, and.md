This edition exists in three distinct states: the first includes copies that were sold without Troilus and Cressida; the second contains Troilus and Cressida but without its prologue and with a redundant final page of Romeo and Juliet crossed out by the printer; the third includes a cancel leaf with the Prologue of Troilus on its recto and the first page of the play, reprinted from a new setting of type, on its verso. In none of the states is Troilus included in the "Catalogue" of plays.

This copy features extensive early annotations by someone who apparently knew some of the actors in the King's Men, Shakespeare's company, and had seen them perform. On the list of principal actors (sig. πA6) the annotator claims to 'know' Robert Benfield, John Lowine 'by eyewitness', and Richard Burbage 'by report'. Beneath William Shakespeare is the intriguing comment 'Leass for making' (perhaps meaning that he, as the maker of plays, may have acted less than other actors). Other annotations record responses to the first few plays in the volume: 'stark naught' for Two Gentlemen of Verona, 'pretty well' for The Tempest, and 'very good, light' for Merry Wives. The annotator characterizes Ford's mistrust of his wife in the latter play as 'a good jealous man's dilemma.' Passages throughout are marked with 'ap.' -- presumably an abbreviation of 'approbo' (I approve).

The early annotator may have been Henry Cary, first Viscount Falkland (1575-1633): his son, Lorenzo Cary (1613-41), wrote his name on C5v. Lorenzo's mother, Elizabeth Cary (1585-1639) was a poet and playwright; her Tragedy of Miriam (1613) is the only known play written by a woman in the period. The Cary family had strong connections in the literary world. Ben Jonson's Epigram 66 is addressed to Henry Cary, and Jonson wrote an ode 'To the Immortal Memory and Friendship' of Lorenzo's elder brother, Lucius. Henry Tozer dedicated his Directions for a Godly Life (1628) to Lorenzo, who had been his student at Exeter College, Oxford. This copy was acquired around 1780 by the fifth Earl of Inchiquin, who had carried the colors in Germany at the Battle of Lauffeld in 1747. A friend of the great Shakespeare editor Edmond Malone, Inchiquin married the niece of the painter Sir Joshua Reynolds. The volume was later briefly owned by another celebrated Shakespeare editor, James Orchard Halliwell-Phillipps.
