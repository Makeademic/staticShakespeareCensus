This edition exists in three distinct states: the first includes copies that were sold without Troilus and Cressida; the second contains Troilus and Cressida but without its prologue and with a redundant final page of Romeo and Juliet crossed out by the printer; the third includes a cancel leaf with the Prologue of Troilus on its recto and the first page of the play, reprinted from a new setting of type, on its verso. In none of the states is Troilus included in the "Catalogue" of plays.

This is the original deposit copy sent by Jaggard, as per Thomas Bodley’s 1610 agreement with the Stationers’ Company, to the Bodleian. The unbound volume was then sent to William Wildgoose on 17 February 1624 as recorded in the Bodleian Binder’s Book .... The general consensus is that upon the acquisition of the Third Folio, with its added plays not found in the First, it was sold as superfluous to the Oxford bookseller Richard Davis in 1664 for £24. Following Davis’ possession of the volume it somehow found its way into the collection of Richard Turbutt (1689-1758) ....The Folio remained in the family until his great-grandson, William Gladwyn Turbutt (1853-1911), instructed his great-great-grandson, Gladwyn Maurice Revel Turbutt (c 1884-1916), to bring it into the Bodleian to have the binding examined for restoration purposes. The volume was quickly identified as the original deposit copy and a race began to reacquire it for the Bodleian...Turbutt declined Sotheran’s offer, on behalf of Henry Clay Folger, and only a few day later sent a telegram in which he agreed to accept £3000 from the Bodleian for the volume.
