This edition exists in three distinct states: the first includes copies that were sold without Troilus and Cressida; the second contains Troilus and Cressida but without its prologue and with a redundant final page of Romeo and Juliet crossed out by the printer; the third includes a cancel leaf with the Prologue of Troilus on its recto and the first page of the play, reprinted from a new setting of type, on its verso. In none of the states is Troilus included in the "Catalogue" of plays.

This copy is an excellent example of the mania in the 18th and 19th centuries to create pristine and uniform copies of the First Folio. The entire volume has been completely inlaid on newer paper and has been extensively washed and pressed. This process likely occurred under the ownership of John Philip Kemble (1757-1823), who made a habit of having the pages of his purchases inlaid. Kemble was a distinguished actor and brother to renowned actress Sarah Siddons. That Kemble is the one who did this is supported by the fact that the watermarks on the paper upon which the original leaves are inlaid indicates the paper was made in 1799. John Henderson (1747-85), who Sidney Lee suggested might have owned the copy before Kemble, could not have inlaid the pages since he died in 1785 ....The volume was also owned by James Boswell the younger (1778-1822), co-editor with Edmond Malone of a revised edition of Shakespeare.
