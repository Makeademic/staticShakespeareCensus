Before Lord Brotherton acquired this copy, it was in the collection of the innovator and entrepreneur Theodore N. Vail. Vail initiated 'Fast Mail', the first mail-only train service, between New York and Chicago. Three years later, he was hired as general manager of the newly formed American Bell Telephone Company, later AT&T. If Alexander Graham Bell invented the telephone, Vail invented the telephone business. As the head of AT&T, Vail oversaw a communications revolution that established the telephone as a necessity of everyday life. He had instituted phone service between New York and San Francisco by 1915, and between America and Europe a year later. Given Vail’s remarkable accomplishments in enabling people to speak to each other across vast distances, it is perhaps not surprising that he purchased a First Folio—arguably the work of the greatest communicator in any language. After Vail’s death in 1920, his First Folio was sold with a Second, Third, and Fourth Folio, on 2 May 1922, at Anderson Galleries. At the sale, Gabriel Wells bought all four Folios for $17,250, paying $9,500 for the First. Lord Brotherton privately purchased the copy in 1924 for an unknown price. It was bequeathed to the University Library by Brotherton on his death in 1930, finally reaching the Library in 1936.
